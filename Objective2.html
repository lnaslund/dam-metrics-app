2a. Meet water demands: In 2015, withdrawals from fresh surface water, including streams, lakes, and reservoirs, accounted for over 60% of total water withdrawals in the US in 2015 (198 billion gallons/day), with the most substantial withdrawals for thermoelectric power, irrigation, and public supply (Dieter et al. 2018).

2b. Meet power demands: Hydropower accounted for 6.3% of power generation in the US in 2021 and is used extensively to provide short-term ramping flexibility (Uría-Martínez et al. 2021, U.S. EIA 2022).

2c. Meet navigational demands: The US government maintains more than 200 locks and dams within its 12,000 miles of inland and intracoastal waterways, making these structures important conduits for commercial navigation (Carter and Fritelli 2020). Some dams are also critical terrestrial transportation structures, with major roads that traverse the dam, such as US 93 which previously crossed the Hoover Dam.

2d. Minimize flood risk: Flood control is the primary use for approximately 20% of the dams listed in the National Inventory of Dams (USACE 2016). This amounts to roughly 16,000 dams. Removal may increase flood risk for downstream floodplain communities that developed with the security afforded by the dam (Habel et al. 2020; Ho et al. 2017).
